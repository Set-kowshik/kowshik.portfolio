Project Overview

Freshly is a conceptual mobile app designed to combat household food waste through intelligent pantry management and proactive recipe inspiration. By transforming a daily chore into a seamless and delightful experience, Freshly helps users save money, reduce their environmental impact, and answer the timeless question: “What’s for dinner?” This case study details the end-to-end design process, from initial research to a high-fidelity, validated prototype.

The Problem

Globally, approximately one-third of all food produced for human consumption is wasted each year . At the household level, this translates to both financial loss and environmental strain. The scale of this issue is immense, as illustrated by the data below.

Data Source: , ,

My initial research identified that this isn’t due to a lack of care, but a combination of common frustrations:

  • Lack of Awareness: Busy lives make it difficult to track what’s in the pantry and when it expires. Food gets pushed to the back of the fridge and forgotten.
  • Friction in Planning: Manually checking ingredients and searching for suitable recipes is a time-consuming chore that often leads to decision fatigue.
  • The “Usage Gap”: Even when people know what they have, they often lack the inspiration to use ingredients—especially disparate ones—before they spoil, leading them to buy more groceries instead.

The core challenge was to design a solution that felt less like a chore and more like a helpful, intelligent kitchen assistant.

Phase 1: Discover – Understanding User Habits

To ground the project in real-world behavior, I began with a multi-faceted research approach.

Competitive Analysis

I analyzed existing solutions, from basic note-taking apps to large recipe platforms like Allrecipes and meal-kit apps like Blue Apron. Many recipe apps and pantry trackers exist, but none fully bridge the gap between a user’s actual inventory and meal inspiration. The following chart highlights this competitive landscape and identifies where Freshly can innovate.

Data Source: , ,

Key Insight: While many apps offered recipes, none effectively closed the loop between a user’s actual home inventory and meal inspiration. The connection was manual and cumbersome. This was the key opportunity for Freshly.

User Interviews

I conducted interviews with 6 individuals who manage their household groceries. My goal was to understand their shopping habits, meal planning processes, and frustrations with food waste. The findings were compiled into an affinity diagram to identify common themes, which are summarized below.

Data Source: Synthesized from user interviews conducted as part of this case study.

Key Quotes:

“I’ll find a shrivelled vegetable in the back of my fridge and feel so guilty. It’s such a waste of money.”
“The hardest part of cooking is just deciding what to make. I have ingredients but no ideas.”
“If tracking my pantry takes more time than just looking, I won’t use it.”

The primary insight was clear: The solution must be built for speed and automation. Any feature requiring significant manual input would fail.

Phase 2: Define – Focusing on the User

With the research data, I moved to synthesize the findings and define a clear direction for the design.

User Persona

I created the persona of “Busy Priya” to represent the target user group. This helped to humanize the problem and maintain focus on user-centric goals throughout the design process.

Persona: Busy Priya

  • Age: 32
  • Occupation: Marketing Manager
  • Goals:
    • Eat healthy, home-cooked meals.
    • Save money on groceries and reduce her environmental footprint.
    • Spend less time stressing about meal planning.
  • Frustrations:
    • Forgetting what she has in her pantry and buying duplicates.
    • Feeling uninspired to cook with the ingredients she has on hand.
    • Hates the guilt of throwing away expired food.

Defining the Problem

Based on Priya’s needs, I formulated a core problem statement:

Busy professionals need an automated and inspiring way to manage their home pantry and plan meals, because the current manual process is time-consuming and leads to significant food and money waste.

This led to several “How Might We” questions to guide brainstorming:

  • How might we make inventory tracking feel magical and effortless?
  • How might we surface the right recipe at the perfect time?
  • How might we motivate users by showing them their positive impact?

Phase 3: Develop – Ideation and Design

This phase focused on turning insights into a tangible solution, starting broad and progressively refining the design.

User Flow

I mapped out the primary user flow: scanning a receipt to populate the pantry, discovering a recipe, and adding missing items to a shopping list. This ensured the app’s architecture was logical and intuitive from the start.

Wireframing

I progressed from low-fidelity sketches to high-fidelity wireframes in Figma. The primary focus was on three key screens:

  • The Onboarding: How to introduce the “magic” of receipt scanning in a clear, trustworthy way.
  • The “Use-It-Up” Feed: Designing a home screen that was dynamic and engaging, prioritizing expiring items.
  • The Shopping List: Creating a list that was intelligent and prevented users from buying things they already owned.

UI Design and Branding

I developed a visual identity for Freshly focused on a clean, vibrant, and encouraging aesthetic. The color palette uses fresh greens and warm, food-inspired accent colors. The typography is clear and legible, creating a calm and user-friendly interface.

Phase 4: Deliver – Testing and Iteration

Before finalizing the design, I conducted usability testing with 5 participants using a high-fidelity Figma prototype. I gave them a simple task: “You just got home from the grocery store. Add your items using this receipt and find a recipe you can make for dinner tonight.”

Usability Testing

Overall, users found the concept intuitive and exciting. However, a few pain points emerged:

  • Receipt Scanning was a “Wow” Moment: Users loved the concept, but wanted to easily edit quantities post-scan.
  • Recipe Filters were Needed: Users wanted to filter the “Use-It-Up” feed by dietary restrictions or meal type (e.g., “vegan,” “quick dinner”).
  • Impact Dashboard was Highly Motivating: Seeing potential savings was a powerful incentive for continued use.

Design Iterations

Based on the feedback, I made a key iteration. I added a quick-edit step after the receipt scan, allowing users to tap on items to adjust quantities or expiry dates before confirming. This addressed the primary friction point found during testing.

The Final Solution

The final design for Freshly provides a seamless and intelligent kitchen management experience. Key features include:

Effortless Onboarding & Inventory

Users are greeted with a simple option to scan a receipt. The app uses OCR to digitize the list, making pantry tracking a ten-second task, not a ten-minute chore . The pantry is automatically populated with items and estimated expiry dates, giving users an instant overview of what they have on hand.

The “Use-It-Up” Feed

The home screen is a personalized feed of beautiful recipe cards. The algorithm prioritizes recipes that use ingredients closest to their expiry date, turning potential waste into a delicious meal. This approach is supported by research indicating that encouraging users to "use up" existing ingredients is a high-impact strategy for reducing food waste.

Data Source:

Each recipe card shows mouthwatering photos, cooking time, and a list of required ingredients that the user already has. Users can filter recipes by diet or meal type to find exactly what they’re craving.

Intelligent Shopping List

When a user finds a recipe they want to cook, any missing ingredients can be one-tap added to the shopping list. The shopping list automatically checks against the user’s pantry. An item already in stock is flagged, preventing duplicate purchases and saving money.

Impact Dashboard

A clean, visual dashboard tracks money saved and food waste prevented, providing positive reinforcement and connecting daily actions to a larger, meaningful goal. This ties into the idea that showing users the tangible, positive impact of their actions (money saved, waste prevented) is a powerful motivator for long-term engagement .

Project Takeaways & Next Steps

What I Learned

  • Automation is the Key to Adoption: For an app to become a daily habit, it must solve a problem with significantly less effort than the user’s current method. The success of Freshly hinges on the “magic” of its automation .
  • Positive Reinforcement Drives Engagement: While solving the problem is key, showing users the tangible, positive impact of their actions (money saved, waste prevented) is a powerful motivator for long-term engagement .

Next Steps

Moving forward, I would like to implement the following improvements and extensions:

  • Expand Automation: Integrate with smart kitchen devices (e.g., smart scales or fridge cameras) for even more hands-free inventory updates.
  • Enhance Personalization: Use machine learning to learn a user’s preferences and better tailor recipe suggestions over time.
  • Community & Sharing: Allow users to share surplus ingredients or recipes with friends and family, further reducing waste on a community level.

By continuing to iterate based on user feedback and emerging technologies, Freshly can evolve into an indispensable smart kitchen companion, helping users save money, eat better, and contribute to a more sustainable world—one meal at a time.
